Intro: Requirements and values [DRAFT]

My productivity system is designed towards the following requirements and principles:

  • speed for adding ideas and tasks at all times;
  • reliability towards others; (i.e. don’t forget & don’t be late on my duties)
  • resilience: I must be able to access my data very easily and quickly, even if I lose access to my accounts (thx to backups), or if I have to switch to another another computer (cloud + multi-platform). While avoiding vendor lock-in. (free data, standard formats)
  • minimalism: I don’t like to be surrounded by things, especially if it’s work in progress. I like a clean workspace.
  • efficient task/project switching, even with bad memory
  • journaling / retrospective-friendly: keep a history of what I did VS what I planned => see how good I did during a week, and improve for next week
  • motivation, even when having to work on boring and/or hard tasks.



Tracy (https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=q_rTEB92jJo) says that a piece of paper (or todolist software) is enough for effective time management. He’s right, but 1- nobody uses todo list software that way (daily list), and 2- he does not say of how to decide of tasks, split them and schedule them.


Problem: I am scared by the length of my ToDo-list

Don’t get me wrong. I still list my ToDo tasks! Just not in the same place.

After months of GTD-inspired usage of Wunderlist, and working on a growing number of projects simultaneously, three issues emerged:

  1. My tasks were scattered between Wunderlist and my email inbox => Sometimes, I was creating redundancy by copying tasks from my email inbox to Wunderlist, and sometimes not.
  2. I ended up with too many heterogeneous tasks of various scale, mixed in just two lists: “Pro” and “Fun activities” => it became hard to have a big-picture view of my progress and next steps for each project.
  3. It was hard to schedule upcoming tasks precisely, based on the amount of time I wanted to invest on each project during a given week.

Also, having switched from an iPhone to a Google phone (Nexus 5x), it become faster to send reminders to Google Inbox, thanks to the provided widget/shortcut. Meaning that all my tasks would end up in one central place: my email inbox.

=> Decision: I add new tasks in Google Inbox.

Problem: I lose track of some of my projects

Take a step back. Isn’t there anything that you started doing some time ago, but ended up leaving out of sight? Like tidying your basement, maybe, or planning your dream trip to South America? Or even something smaller like sorting your photo albums? Well, these kinds of tasks are easily forgotten because we tend to follow one single routine that we see as more critical. E.g. our daily job and/or duties.

When you want to create a job out of the things you’re passionate about, you need to make an inventory of all these projects, make a selection, and define an execution plan for each of them.

Recently, I decided to stop working on anything but my own projects. Instead of focusing on just one and hope that it succeeds, I’m embracing the uncertainty of my investments (i.e. like most startup investors do), by bootstrapping several ideas at once.

That means that I’m working on several projects simultaneously, by splitting my time between them. I’m currently working on eleven projects.

And I figured that Trello was a good tool for making an inventory:

Besides making my project list look great, Trello cards (i.e. one card per project) have been very efficient:

  • to maintain a list of links to all the resources that a project relies upon, e.g.: specifications & design documents, screenshots, passwords…
  • to keep a history of changes (e.g. bug fixes, releases, product decisions) and events (e.g. successes, media coverage, crashes) related to the project;
  • and to hold project-based task lists (a.k.a. “Checklists” in Trello), to always know what was the last task I got done, and what’s the next one.

With this use of Trello, each card implements what Matthieu Leventis calls a project journal.

That way, when I get back to a project after a few days, I can very quickly put my mind back to where I left off, and go on from there.

=> Decision: I store the progress and next steps of my projects in Trello.

Problem: I didn’t have time to do XYZ

Come on, you know that this is bullshit.

We always have time for things that matter. The keys are to decide how much time we want to invest on what, and to know what to do when!

Ok, this was not very clear… Let me illustrate my case, for instance.

Now that I have listed the projects I want to work on, I need to decide how many hours I want to invest to each project, and then, allocate time-slots for them.

I like to make weekly schedules on Sundays, so that I can start working immediately and mindlessly during the following week, starting on Monday morning.

  • need visibility of what I’m doing when, and how long => scheduling in calendar
  • control time investment => scheduling per project => getplan

=> Decision: I schedule and time-track tasks on Google Calendar.


Problem: I’m anxious / tired / working too much

=> Decision: I log my progress and motivate myself using a self-journal.


Wishlist

  • a way to transfer my next steps from trello to my calendar more smoothly
  • a way to compare how many hours I spend on what VS how many I intended to spend on what => see performance
  • a way to know which kind of tasks too longer than expected
  • a way to know which kind of tasks I kept pushing (or cancelling)
